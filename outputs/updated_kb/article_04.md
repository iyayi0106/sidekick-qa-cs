WHAT IS ATTRITION?
Attrition is the minimum percentage of your room block that must be filled. If your group doesn't meet this
threshold, you (or your client) pay penalties for the shortfall.
HOW ATTRITION WORKS
Example:
— You book a block of 30 rooms
— Contract has 15% attrition
— You must fill at least 26 rooms (30 - 15% = 26 rooms minimum)
— If you only fill 24 rooms, you owe payment for 2 additional rooms
STANDARD ATTRITION RATES
Industry standard: 20-30% attrition (per Article 1)
Most hotels will offer attrition in this range. The lower the attrition percentage, the more rooms you must fill.
What different attrition rates mean:
— 10% attrition = must fill 90% of block (higher risk)
— 15% attrition = must fill 85% of block (recommended)
— 20% attrition = must fill 80% of block (more conservative)
— 25% attrition = must fill 75% of block (lower risk, but may cost more)
RECOMMENDED ATTRITION BY GROUP TYPE
Weddings
Recommended: 20-25% attrition for weddings (see Article 1; do not push 15% aggressively)
Weddings usually have firm headcounts. Guest lists are finalized months in advance, and attendance is predictable.
Corporate Groups
Recommended: 20-25% attrition for corporate groups
Corporate groups typically have confirmed attendees, but last-minute changes can happen (business needs,
schedule conflicts).
Reunions and Social Groups
Recommended: 18-20% attrition
Social groups have more uncertainty. People may drop out closer to the event date.
Incentive Groups
Recommended: 18-22% attrition
Incentive travel groups are usually fully funded by the company, so attendance is high.
Conferences
Recommended: 20-25% attrition for corporate groups
Conferences depend on registration success. Build in some buffer.
NEGOTIATING ATTRITION
Push for lower attrition only when group is highly committed AND expert-approved:
— You're confident about group size
— Group has high commitment (deposits already collected)
— Peak season (hotel wants the business locked in)
Lower attrition may result in higher room rates or fewer concessions—not always 'better rates'
Hotels offer lower pricing when you take on more risk.
Push for higher attrition (lower fill requirement) when:
— Group size is uncertain
— Event is far out (12+ months)
— Client hasn't collected deposits yet
— Shoulder season or low demand period
Higher attrition = More protection, but may cost more
RED FLAGS
Attrition below 10%: Too risky. Pushes all risk onto you. Avoid unless you're 100% certain about attendance.
Attrition above 30%: Hotel is protecting themselves too much. You should be able to negotiate better terms.
No attrition clause: Rare, but some courtesy blocks don't have attrition. This means you can walk away penalty-free
if the group doesn't fill.
CALCULATING ATTRITION RISK
Before accepting an attrition clause, calculate your worst-case scenario:
Example:
— 40-room block
— Room rate: $250/night
— 3-night stay
— 15% attrition (must fill 34 rooms)
Worst case: Only 30 rooms fill
— Shortfall: 4 rooms
— Penalty: 4 rooms × $250 × 3 nights = $3,000
Is your client (or you) willing to risk $3,000? If not, negotiate higher attrition.
ATTRITION AND CUT-OFF DATES
Attrition is typically calculated at the cut-off date, not at check-in.
Important: If your group hasn't reached the minimum by cut-off, you owe payment for the shortfall—even if the
hotel re-sells those rooms.
Some contracts allow you to adjust your block size down before cut-off without penalty. Always check this.
PROTECTING YOURSELF
1. Collect deposits from travelers early
Don't commit to a group block until you have confirmed interest and deposits from enough travelers to meet
attrition.
2. Set an internal cut-off earlier than hotel cut-off
If the hotel cut-off is 45 days out, set your client deadline at 55-60 days. This gives you time to adjust.
3. Negotiate sliding attrition
Some hotels will agree to adjust attrition based on how far out the event is:
— 12 months out: 20% attrition
— 6 months out: 15% attrition
— 3 months out: 10% attrition
This protects you early while locking in lower rates closer to the event.
4. Get attrition terms in writing
Make sure the contract clearly states:
— Attrition percentage
— How attrition is calculated (rooms? room nights? revenue?)
— When attrition is assessed (cut-off date? check-in?)
— Penalty structure
WHAT HAPPENS IF YOU DON'T MEET ATTRITION?
Scenario 1: You pay for unfilled rooms
Most common. You (or the client) pay the contracted rate for rooms that didn't fill.
Scenario 2: Attrition based on revenue, not rooms
Some contracts calculate attrition on total revenue. If your group upgrades to suites, you might meet revenue
attrition even with fewer rooms.
Scenario 3: Hotel resells rooms and waives penalty
Rare, but some contracts allow this. If the hotel re-sells your released rooms, they may waive the penalty. Don't
count on this.
COMMUNICATING ATTRITION TO CLIENTS
Always explain attrition clearly to clients:
> "We've secured a block of 30 rooms with 15% attrition. This means we need to fill at least 26 rooms by [cut-off
date]. If we don't reach 26 rooms, there will be a penalty of $[X] per unfilled room. Let's set a goal of 28 rooms to
give ourselves a buffer."
Transparency builds trust and ensures clients understand their responsibility.
COMMON QUESTIONS
Can I reduce my room block before cut-off?
Sometimes. Many contracts allow a one-time reduction (often 10-20% of original block) without penalty if done
60-90 days before arrival.
What if my group grows beyond the block size?
Hotels usually accommodate overflow at the group rate (subject to availability). Get this in writing.
Does attrition apply to comp rooms?
Usually no. Complimentary rooms typically don't count toward attrition calculations.
KEY TAKEAWAYS
— Aim for 20-30% attrition unless expert guidance says otherwise for most groups
— Lower attrition may result in higher room rates or fewer concessions—not always 'better rates', but higher risk
— Always calculate worst-case penalty before signing
— Collect traveler deposits early to reduce your risk
— Communicate attrition clearly to clients
